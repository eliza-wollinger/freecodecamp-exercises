1879

On March 14, Hermann Einstein, an engineer and salesman, and Pauline Einstein had their first child in Ulm, German Empire. And they called him Albert Einstein.

At the age of 16, two years before the standard, he took the entrance exams for the Swiss Federal Polytechnic School, even though he did not reach the required standard in some subjects, he obtained exceptional marks in physics and mathematics. Following the advice of the director of the Polytechnic, he attended a Swiss school to complete high school, and the following year he began his studies at college.

1895

1900

Einstein got his bachelor's degree in physics and mathematics. But during his graduation, he missed his classes to stay in the labs, and he never paid much attention to his professors. And so, he was even turned down for a job as a lab assistant. He ended up getting a job at the Swiss Patent Office, which he got with the help of a friend of his father.

Many disbelieved that a public servant could do science. But this was Einstein's miraculous year, where he published not just one, but four works that would change human history.

1905

The first work was released in March, and in it there was a shocking hypothesis, despite decades of evidence that light was a wave, Einstein proposed that it was actually a particle. Showing how mysterious phenomena such as the photoelectric effect could be explained by this hypothesis.

This idea was ridiculed for a long time, but after 20 years, the wave-particle duality would become the basis for the quantum revolution.

Two months later, in May, he did a study on the existence of atoms, although some theories were based on the idea of invisible atoms, many scientists still believed it was fiction. But Eisstein used a clever argument to convince them: he showed that the behavior of small particles moving randomly in a liquid medium, known as Brownian motion, could be predicted by the collision of millions of invisible atoms.

The third work was published in June, and it came about due to a problem Einstein had with the inconsistency between two fundamental principles of physics: the principle of relativity, said that absolute motion could not be defined, but in electromagnetic theory, it stated that he existed. The discrepancy between these questions brought Einstein into what he called psychic tension. After much debate on the subject, Einstein realized that this contradiction could be resolved if the speed of light remained constant, regardless of the frame of reference adopted. And he formulated what became known as special relativity.

In September he published his fourth work, which was a kind of complement to the work of relativity. Taking a closer look at his earlier work, Albert realized that it also meant that mass and energy were equivalent. And his relationship could be represented by the equation that marked his life and history:

1908

In February, Einstein was already considered an important scientist, being invited to teach at important colleges.

He was invited to participate in the first Solvay Conference in Brussels, which brought together some of the greatest scientists of all times, along with Max Planck and Marie Curie.

1911

1916

He was appointed president of the German Society of Physics, a position he would hold until 1918.

Einstein was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics "for his contributions to theoretical physics and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect". Relativity was not well understood.

1922

1925

Received the Copley Medal from the Royal Society, and the next year, Gold Medal from the Royal Astronomical Society.

During a visit to the United States, Einstein decided not to return to Germany due to the Nazi Party's rise to power with its new chancellor Adolf Hitler. But after a while he returned by ship to Belgium at the end of March, during the voyage he was informed that his home had been invaded by the Nazis and his personal sailboat confiscated. Upon disembarking, he immediately went to the German consulate where he presented his passport and formally renounced German citizenship. On the same day he sent a letter in which he submitted his resignation to the Prussian Academy in Berlin. In early April, he learned that the new German government had instituted laws that prohibited Jews from holding official positions, including teaching at universities.

1933

Historian Gerald Holton has described that "virtually no sound protests were raised by their colleagues", thousands of Jewish scientists were suddenly forced to give up their university positions and their names were removed from the lists of institutions in which they were employed. A month later, Einstein's works were among the Nazi book-burning targets, and the Joseph Goebbels Propaganda Ministry proclaimed, "Jewish intellectualism is dead." Einstein also learned that his name was on an assassination target list, with a "$5,000 bounty on his head." A German magazine included him in a list of enemies of the regime with the phrase "not yet hanged".

1935

Albert decided to stay permanently in the United States and apply for US citizenship. Princeton Institute for Advanced Study, and his affiliation would last until his death in 1955. He never returned to Europe.

A group of Hungarian scientists tried to alert Washington of ongoing Nazi research into the atomic bomb. Group notices were ignored. Einstein found one of the scientists, and the two "considered it their responsibility to alert Americans to the possibility that German scientists might win the race to build an atomic bomb, and to warn that Hitler would be more than willing to resort to such a weapon".

1950

Einstein discovered an aneurysm, but refuses treatment, according to him "it was bad taste to prolong life artificially" and had already accomplished his mission.
